bipolar disoder

Bipolar disorder is a treatable illness in which a person experiences extreme mood swings and activity levels. These are different from the usual ups and downs that everyone feels because they cause serious impairment in function. Usually, sufferers have both “manic episodes”, with a high mood and increased levels of energy and activity, and “depressed episodes” in which their mood is low and their energy and activity levels drop.

what are the symptoms of bipolar disorder?

Bipolar disorder involves episodes of mania and depression. Symptoms that happen over a certain period are called “episodes”.Feelings are intense and happen with changes in behavior, energy levels, or activity levels that are noticeable to others.

manic episodes

  1. elevated mood
  2. Decreased need for sleep.
  3. excessive irritability, anger or rage.
  4. Difficulties in sleeping.
  5. Trouble concentrating.
  6. Excessive appetite for food, drinking, sex, or other pleasurable activities.
  7. Talking fast about a lot of different things (“flight of ideas”).

Depressive episodes

  1. sadness.
  2. losing interest in the things one used to enjoy.
  3. Recurrent nightmares and flashbacks.
  4. Difficulties in sleeping.
  5. low self-esteem
  6. Feeling slowed down or restless
  7. thoughts of self-harm and suicide.
  8. feeling guilty or worthless.

What causes bipolar disorder?

The exact cause that leads to some people developing bipolar disorder is not known, but there are factors that play a part in causing the illness or triggering it

Genes :

Bipolar disorder often runs in families, and research suggests this is mostly explained by heredity.

Brain Structure and Function:

Research shows that the brain structure and function of people with bipolar disorder may differ from those of people who do not have bipolar disorder or other mental disorders.

How is bipolar disorder treated?

Mental health professionals treat bipolar disorder with medications, psychotherapy, or a combination of treatments. Medical treatment may involve administering mood stabilizers such as lithium or valproate.Medications that target sleep or anxiety are sometimes added to mood stabilizers as part of a treatment plan.

Psychotherapy (sometimes called “talk therapy”) is a term for various treatment techniques that aim to help a person identify and change troubling emotions, thoughts, and behaviors. Psychotherapy can offer support, education, skills, and strategies to people with bipolar disorder and their families.

Coping With Bipolar Disorder

Living with bipolar disorder can be challenging, but there are ways to help yourself, as well as your friends and loved ones.

  1. Get treatment and stick with it. Treatment is the best way to start feeling better.
  2. Keep medical and therapy appointments and talk with your health care provider about treatment options.
  3. Regular, vigorous exercise like jogging, swimming, or bicycling, which can help with depression and anxiety, promote better sleep, and is healthy for your heart and brain.
  4. Keep a life chart to help recognize your mood swings.
  5. cognitive behavioural therapy-which looks at ways to change negative patterns in feelings, thoughts and behaviour.
  6. Interpersonal therapy-which focuses on relationships with others and how these relationships relate to feelings, thoughts and behaviour

During an episode of mania or hypomania, psychological treatments are not usually advisable rather they need to have access to calming environments and reduced stimulation.It is also advisable not to make important decision at this point until they have recovered from their episode.If people are experiencing a long and severe period of depression or a long period of mania and other treatments have not worked, or the situation is life-threatening, electroconvulsive therapy may be considered.
